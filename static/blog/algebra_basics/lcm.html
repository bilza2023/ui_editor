Lowest Common Multiple (LCM)

What is LCM?

The Lowest Common Multiple (LCM) of two or more numbers is the smallest number that is a multiple of all of them.

LCM is useful when we want to find a common time or unit â€” for example, when two events happen on different schedules and we want to know when they will happen together.

Methods to Find LCM

Method 1: Listing Multiples

Write down multiples of each number until you find the first one they have in common.

Example:

  • Multiples of 4: 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, ...
  • Multiples of 6: 6, 12, 18, 24, ...
  • LCM = 12

Method 2: Prime Factorization

Break each number into prime factors. Take the highest powers of all primes involved.

Example:

  • \( 12 = 2^2 \times 3 \)
  • \( 15 = 3 \times 5 \)
  • \( \text{LCM} = 2^2 \times 3 \times 5 = 60 \)

Method 3: Division Ladder (Short Division)

Divide numbers together by common primes, then multiply all the divisors.

Examples

  • LCM of 4 and 6 is \( 12 \)
  • LCM of 10 and 12 is \( 60 \)
  • LCM of 5, 6, and 8 is \( 120 \)
  • LCM of 3, 4, 9: \( 3 = 3,\ 4 = 2^2,\ 9 = 3^2 \Rightarrow \text{LCM} = 2^2 \times 3^2 = 36 \)

Applications of LCM

  • Common Denominators: In fractions, LCM helps align denominators.
  • Time Scheduling: Two lights blink every 5 and 6 seconds. When together? â†’ LCM = 30 sec
  • Math Word Problems: Least quantity to buy, minimum time intervals, etc.
